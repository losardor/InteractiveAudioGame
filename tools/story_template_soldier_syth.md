# The Soldier and the Sìthean

## Meta
**Setting:** A remote island called Eilean Garbh, thrust from the Atlantic like the spine of some drowned creature. Part of an archipelago battered raw by gales that strip the heather to the root. The coastline gnaws at itself — black crags bitten by salt spray, caves that swallow the tide and spit it back as thunder. Inland, deep glens cut through the rock where life huddles and persists: stone cottages with turf roofs, a scattering of sheep, and gardens clawed from peat. The wind never rests. It howls through the glen like something searching.
**Listener role:** Callum. A soldier returned from a nameless continental war, missing his left leg below the knee. He sits in his mother's cottage, whittling sticks into nothing, while his family orbits him with care he cannot bear to accept. His dog, a grey lurcher named Bran, lies at his feet. Callum is drowning in the debt of other people's kindness and the uselessness of his own hands.
**Central question:** What does a broken man owe to those who mend him? And what happens when the price of wholeness is paid by the community that held you together?
**Echo:** In Choice 2, a hare flees across Callum's path, chased by Bran. If Callum whistles Bran back and spares the creature, the hare returns in Choice 5 — revealed as a messenger of the Sìth, carrying knowledge that reshapes the final choice.

## Choice 1

### Soundscape
Wind keening across open moorland, constant and shifting in pitch. Distant crash of surf against rock. A wooden door creaks open. Uneven footsteps on stone — one boot, one wooden peg — tap-thud, tap-thud. A dog's claws click on flagstone, then soft panting. Birdsong, sparse — a curlew's cry threading through the wind.

### Narration
The door groaned against its frame, and the wind seized it, flinging it wide as if the island itself beckoned him out.

Callum stood on the threshold of his mother's cottage and gripped the jamb with both hands. Three months he had lain in that bed. Three months of broth spooned to his lips, of his sister's fingers rewrapping the stump where his left leg ended, of his brother shouldering the work that once split between two. Three months of kindness so thick it choked him.

Bran pressed his muzzle into Callum's palm and whined.

"Aye," Callum muttered. "Out."

He descended the two stone steps — a negotiation with gravity that cost him his pride each time — and stood in the yard. The sky hung iron-grey, bruised with weather rolling in from the west. Before him, two paths split from the cottage gate.

To the left, the glen path wound down through a crease in the hillside. Rowan trees arched above it, their branches laced together like the fingers of old women praying. Moss carpeted every stone. The burn chattered somewhere below, hidden by fern and foxglove. It was the path to the village, to the harbour, to every face that would greet him with pity wrung into a smile.

To the right, the shore path climbed over the headland and dropped to the black rocks where the Atlantic hammered itself white. No trees grew there. No shelter. Just the sky and the sea and the wind that scoured everything clean. No one walked the shore path in winter unless they wanted to be alone with something they could not name.

But the dog was fast, and the hare was flagging, and there was something in Callum that wanted to see one creature in this place act on its nature without apology.

## Choice 3

### Soundscape
Dusk falls. The soundscape darkens. Wind dies to a hush. The burn's voice drops to a murmur. Then — music. Faint, impossible, coming from the hillside above the glen. A melody played on no instrument Callum can name: part fiddle, part voice, part the vibration of the earth itself. It is beautiful. It aches. It pulls at the marrow of his bones. Simultaneously: the sound distorts, warps, takes on an undertone of something mechanical, insectile, wrong. The two qualities coexist — the listener must choose which to believe.

### Narration
Night crept across the glen like a tide filling a pool. The last light bled from the sky behind Sgurr nan Sian, and the fairy hill turned black against the gloaming.

Callum should have turned home. His stump ached. His mother would be ladling broth into bowls, counting the chairs, noting his absence with a worry she wore like a second skin. But the music held him.

It rose from the hillside above — from the tumbled stones and lichen-crusted rock where the old folk swore the Sìthean kept their court. A melody that wrapped around his ribs and squeezed. It spoke of wholeness, of a body unmarred, of legs that carried a man wherever his heart commanded. It promised, without words, that what was broken could be reforged.

But there was something else beneath the beauty. A second voice, threaded through the first like a worm through an apple. A wrongness. The melody bent at angles that no human throat could shape. The sweetness curdled at its edges, and Callum could not tell whether the chill on his neck was awe or warning.

Two truths hung in the air, and both were real.

The music of the Sìth: was it a gift offered, or a trap baited?

## Choice 4

### Soundscape
Interior of the Sìthean — a vast underground hall. Dripping water echoes in stone chambers. The music has ceased. Silence, heavy and deliberate. A voice speaks — neither male nor female, old and young at once, resonant as a struck bell. No ambient warmth or menace: the sound is stripped bare, neutral, waiting. When Callum's choice is made, the thing he surrenders should vanish from the soundscape — a specific absence the listener can feel.

### Narration
The hall stretched beyond the reach of the light. Pillars of black stone rose into a darkness that breathed. The floor was glass-smooth and cold through the sole of his remaining boot.

At the hall's centre, a figure sat on a throne carved from a single piece of quartz. It glowed with its own pale light, and the figure upon it shifted between forms like smoke caught in a draught: now a woman with hair like white fire, now an old man whose eyes were two black wells, now a child with a smile that belonged to no child that ever drew breath.

Bran cowered at Callum's heel, a low whimper threading from his throat.

The figure spoke, and the hall rang with it.

"You seek what was taken from you. A leg. A life. A place among your own. We can give you this. We have given such things before. But we are not merchants who trade in coin. We trade in what matters."

The figure's form settled — a woman now, pale as birch bark, her eyes the colour of winter sea.

"Two prices lie before you. Choose one, or turn back to the world above with nothing changed."

She raised her left hand. "Your memory of home. The faces of your mother, your sister, your brother. The taste of your mother's broth. The sound of the burn below the cottage. Every thread that binds you to Eilean Garbh. You will walk whole, but you will not remember why you wished to walk."

She raised her right hand. "Your hound's devotion. The creature at your heel who followed you to war and back, who lay beside your sickbed and pressed his warmth against your wound. He will live, but he will not know you. You will be a stranger to the one soul that asked nothing of you."

The silence that followed had weight. Callum felt it settle on his shoulders like a yoke.

## Ending A
**Reached by:** Trusted the fairy song (Choice 3A) + Sacrificed memory (Choice 4A) + Accepted the bargain (Choice 5A)

### Soundscape
Dawn wind, subdued. Harbour sounds: water lapping against stone, the creak of a boat's mooring rope. Seagulls. Callum's footsteps — two boots, even and whole — on the harbour cobbles. Coins clink in his pocket. Voices call his name from the cottage on the hill, but they sound muffled, as though reaching him through water. A boat's sail snaps taut. The sounds of the island recede as the boat pulls away. The wind fills the void.

### Narration
He walked through the village on two good legs and the faces that turned to greet him split with joy. His mother seized his hands and searched his eyes and found something missing there, though she could not name it.

"Callum," she breathed. "Callum, you're whole."

"Aye," he said. "Whole."

But the word rang hollow. He stood in the cottage and it was just a cottage — stone walls, a peat fire, a table scored with knife marks. He knew these things the way a man knows a painting: flat, contained, framed behind glass. His sister sang to the sheep and he heard the notes but not the meaning. His brother clapped his shoulder and he felt the weight but not the warmth.

The gold bought a boat. A fine one, with a copper-riveted hull and a sail that caught the wind like a cupped hand. He loaded it with provisions and stood at the harbour wall and could think of no reason to stay.

"Where will you go?" his mother asked, and her voice broke on the question.

"I don't know," he said, and meant it as he had never meant anything in his life.

The boat carried him south. The island shrank behind him — green, grey, a smudge, a memory he could not reach. Ahead, the sea stretched toward continents that promised nothing but novelty, and Callum stood at the prow on two strong legs and felt the wind fill the emptiness where his home had been.

He was whole. He was wealthy. He was free. He was alone in a way that no wealth could furnish, no wholeness could mend.

### Final sound
The cry of a curlew, unanswered.

## Ending B
**Reached by:** Trusted own senses (Choice 3B) + Sacrificed Bran (Choice 4B) + Refused the bargain (Choice 5B)

### Soundscape
Morning on the island, rich and full. The burn's voice, the wind in heather, sheep bleating. Children's voices, first one, then several, asking questions, laughing. Chalk on slate. Callum's uneven footstep on a wooden floor — a schoolhouse. The creak of a chair as he sits. A child reads aloud, stumbling over the words. Callum's voice, patient, guiding. ### Narration
He returned to the cottage at dawn and his mother dropped the kettle at the sight of him.

"The leg —" she began.

"Stays gone," he said. "The leg stays gone. But I'm back."

She held him and he held her and neither spoke for a long while. Outside, the burn ran and the wind blew and the island turned beneath the sky as it had always turned.

He did not speak of Bran. When his sister asked, he said the dog had run off in the night, and she searched his face and knew it for a lie, and had the grace to let it stand.

The months turned. Callum carved himself a better crutch from ash wood, shaped it with the patience of a man who has learned what haste costs. He sat in the village square and the children gathered around him as children will gather around anyone with a story, and he discovered that the war had given him something besides a wound.

He could teach. The campaigns had taught him geography no schoolbook held. The field hospitals had taught him the body's machinery. The fairy hill had taught him that the world contained more than the eye could weigh, and that a man who understood its hidden laws could steer a truer course through it.

The island had no school. It had Callum.

He taught the children letters and sums and the names of the stars that hung above the harbour. He taught them which mushrooms would feed a family and which would fell one. He taught them the old stories — the true ones, where the fairies were not kind and the hero did not always win, and the price of power was always paid by someone who had not agreed to pay it.

At night, alone in the cottage, he wrote. The pen scratched across the paper and he set down everything: the hill, the hall, the woman on the quartz throne, the bargain he had refused, the dog he had lost. He wrote it for the children, and for the children after them, and for every soul on Eilean Garbh who might one day hear music from the hillside and mistake it for a gift.

His leg did not grow back. But he stood, in time, on ground that held him, and the ground was the trust of his people, and it bore his weight.

### Narration
He walked out of the hill on two good legs into a morning he did not recognise.

The island spread before him — green and grey, salt-scoured, ancient. He knew its name. He knew it was the place he had come from, the way a man knows the name of a town he passed through once in childhood. A fact, correctly filed, emptied of weight.

The cottage was below. Smoke rose from its chimney. He watched it and waited for something to move in his chest, and nothing moved.

He descended. The door opened before he reached it and a woman stood in the frame — older than he expected, her hands dusted with flour, her face doing something complicated when she saw him walk whole down the hillside. She said his name. He heard it as sounds, correctly sequenced.

"The leg," she said. "Callum. Your leg."

"Aye," he said. "It's back."

She reached for him and he let her hold him, and he set his hands against her back and understood that he was embracing his mother without being able to feel the truth of it. She wept into his shoulder. He looked past her at the interior of the cottage — the peat fire, the table, the scored wood, the smell of broth — and catalogued it all correctly and felt none of it land.

A man came out behind her. His brother. A woman behind him. His sister. He knew their faces the way he knew the island's name: accurately, and from a great distance.

They asked about Bran. He said the dog had run off in the night. His sister searched his face and he gave her nothing to find, because there was nothing to find, and she turned away with a grief she couldn't account for.

He had the leg. He had traded his home to keep the dog and then lost the dog for naming what he wanted without knowing the law. He had arrived at the exit with the arithmetic correct and every variable wrong.

His mother spooned broth into a bowl and set it before him. He ate it. It tasted of peat and onion and a word in a language he had once spoken.

The island needed tending. He tended it. He worked the croft alongside his brother with a body that was whole and hands that were competent and a silence his family learned to navigate around. He was present in the way a well-made tool is present — available, functional, without remainder.

At night he sat at the door and looked at the hillside. The standing stones were still. No light moved in the dark. He felt no pull toward them, no fear, no unfinished business. Just a man on a threshold, looking at a hill, with two legs and nothing behind his eyes that the hill would recognise.

There is a kind of loss that doesn't announce itself. It doesn't arrive as grief or rage or the long howl of the dispossessed. It arrives as competence. As getting on with things. As a man who works hard and speaks little and never quite meets your eye when you tell him you're glad he's home.

Callum was home. He was whole. The debt was paid in full, and he had no means left to feel it.

### Final sound
Callum's even footsteps — two boots, no tap-thud — crossing the cottage threshold. The door closes. Silence.
